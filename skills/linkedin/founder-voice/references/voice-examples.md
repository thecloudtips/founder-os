# Voice Examples: Before & After Rewrites

Concrete examples showing how to transform generic LinkedIn content into founder voice. Each example demonstrates one or more principles from the Founder Voice skill.

---

## Example 1: Generic Announcement to Founder Story

### Before

> Excited to announce that our company has successfully completed a major product milestone! After months of hard work, our team has delivered a comprehensive solution that will help businesses streamline their operations. We couldn't have done it without our amazing team. Stay tuned for more updates!

### After

> We shipped the feature that almost killed us.

> 4 months. 3 rewrites. 1 engineer quit mid-sprint (unrelated, but the timing was brutal).

> The feature? Automated invoice reconciliation. Not sexy. But our customers were spending 6 hours a week matching payments to invoices manually. Now it takes 10 minutes.

> Sometimes the features that matter most are the ones nobody posts about on LinkedIn.

### What Changed

Replaced the generic "excited to announce" frame with a specific, honest story. Named the actual feature instead of hiding behind "comprehensive solution." Added real numbers (4 months, 3 rewrites, 6 hours to 10 minutes) that make the achievement tangible. The vulnerability about the engineer quitting creates authenticity that "amazing team" never could.

## Example 2: Corporate Update to Opinionated Take

### Before

> As the industry continues to evolve, it's becoming increasingly important for businesses to consider implementing AI solutions in their workflows. Companies that embrace this technology early may find themselves with a competitive advantage in the marketplace. We are proud to be at the forefront of this transformation.

### After

> Most companies are implementing AI wrong.

> They start with "What can AI do?" instead of "What is wasting our team's time?"

> We did it backwards too. Spent 3 months building an AI chatbot nobody used. Then we asked our ops team what they hated doing. Answer: formatting client reports. 45 minutes each, 12 per week.

> Built a simple AI template filler in a weekend. Saves 9 hours a week. No chatbot needed.

> AI is not a strategy. It is a tool. Start with the pain, not the technology.

### What Changed

Flipped from passive industry commentary ("the industry continues to evolve") to a direct, opinionated opening line. Replaced corporate positioning ("proud to be at the forefront") with an honest admission of getting it wrong first. Added specific numbers that ground the take. The closing line delivers a clear, quotable opinion instead of vague competitive advantage language.

## Example 3: Listicle With No Personality to Listicle With Founder Voice

### Before

> 5 Tips for Better Remote Team Management:
>
> 1. Set clear expectations
> 2. Use the right tools
> 3. Schedule regular check-ins
> 4. Build team culture
> 5. Trust your employees
>
> What tips would you add? Comment below!

### After

> I manage a remote team of 14 across 4 time zones.

> Here are 3 things that actually moved the needle (and 2 things everyone recommends that did not work for us):

> What works:
> - Daily async standups in Notion, not Slack. Slack standup messages disappear. Notion entries become a searchable log. We reference them in every 1:1.
> - One "overlap hour" per day (2 PM UTC). Not a meeting. Just everyone online at the same time. Questions get answered in minutes instead of overnight.
> - Monthly "work out loud" sessions where someone shares their screen and narrates their process. We caught 3 major workflow bottlenecks this way.

> What did not work:
> - "Virtual water coolers." We tried Donut, random coffee chats, game hours. Nobody enjoyed them. Forced fun is still forced.
> - Weekly all-hands. Replaced it with a 5-minute Loom from me every Monday. Engagement went up. Meeting fatigue went down.

### What Changed

Replaced generic advice with specific, experience-backed practices. Cut from 5 vague tips to 3 detailed wins and 2 honest failures. Named actual tools (Notion, Slack, Donut, Loom) instead of "use the right tools." Added outcomes for each point. The willingness to share what did not work creates more credibility than five safe tips ever could. Replaced engagement bait ("Comment below!") with content that earns engagement on its own.

## Example 4: Bland Lesson to Authentic Personal Lesson

### Before

> Failure is a stepping stone to success. As entrepreneurs, we must embrace challenges and learn from our mistakes. Every setback is an opportunity for growth. Remember, the most successful founders have all faced significant obstacles on their journey. Keep pushing forward!

### After

> Last year I lost a $180k contract because I sent the proposal 2 hours late.

> Not 2 days late. 2 hours. The client had already signed with someone else.

> What it taught me: speed is a feature. Not just in product. In every client-facing interaction. The fastest reply wins more deals than the best reply.

> Changed 3 things after that:
> - Proposal templates ready for our top 5 project types
> - 24-hour turnaround policy on all client requests
> - Automated follow-up sequences so nothing sits in a queue

> Lost one deal. Fixed the pipeline forever.

### What Changed

Replaced motivational platitudes ("failure is a stepping stone") with a specific, painful loss -- $180k and a 2-hour margin. The lesson emerges from the story instead of being stated as abstract wisdom. Added three concrete changes made as a result, giving the reader something actionable. The closing line ("Lost one deal. Fixed the pipeline forever.") delivers the lesson with short-sentence rhythm instead of generic encouragement.

## Example 5: Humblebrag to Value-First Share

### Before

> Humbled and honored to share that I've been named to the Forbes 30 Under 30 list this year! Never in my wildest dreams did I imagine this would happen. So grateful for this incredible journey and all the people who believed in me along the way. Blessed beyond words. #Grateful #Forbes30Under30 #Blessed #Entrepreneur #StartupLife #Founder #DreamBig

### After

> Made the Forbes 30 Under 30 list. Took me a second to process it.

> But here is the thing nobody tells you about these lists: they do not change your business. Monday morning after the announcement, I still had the same 3 problems I had Friday:
> - A churn rate that needs to drop 2 points
> - A sales hire that is not working out
> - A product roadmap with more items than engineering capacity

> The list got me 47 new LinkedIn connections and zero new customers.

> Recognition is nice. But if you are building something, keep your head in the metrics, not the press.

### What Changed

Stated the achievement plainly in one sentence instead of wrapping it in performed humility. Immediately pivoted to value: what these lists actually do (and do not do) for a business. Named specific, real problems that did not go away -- giving the reader an honest look behind the curtain. Replaced 7 hashtags with zero. The closing line delivers a useful takeaway instead of self-congratulation.

## Example 6: Engagement Bait to Genuine Engagement

### Before

> Agree or disagree?
>
> Hard work beats talent when talent doesn't work hard.
>
> Like if you agree! Repost if this inspired you! Comment your favorite motivational quote below! Follow me for more daily inspiration!
>
> #Motivation #Hustle #Grind #Success #Entrepreneur #MindsetMatters #NeverGiveUp #BossLife

### After

> "Hard work beats talent" is the most misused quote in business.

> It is true in sports. In a 100m sprint, work ethic can close the gap.

> In business? Not always. I have watched founders work 80-hour weeks on the wrong problem. I have done it myself. Twice.

> What actually beats talent: working on the right thing, at the right time, with enough intensity to learn faster than the competition.

> The founders I know who built something real did not just work hard. They quit the wrong things fast enough to find the right things.

> Curious -- what is the one thing you stopped doing that had the biggest impact on your business?

### What Changed

Took the generic quote and added a genuine, experience-backed opinion about why it is misleading. Replaced empty engagement prompts ("Like if you agree!") with a specific, interesting question that invites real responses. Cut 8 hashtags to zero. Added personal credibility ("I have done it myself. Twice.") instead of performing motivation. The closing question earns engagement by being genuinely interesting, not by begging for it.

## Example 7: Product Launch Announcement to Founder Narrative

### Before

> We are thrilled to launch our new product today! After extensive research and development, we have created a cutting-edge solution that leverages the latest technology to deliver best-in-class results. Our innovative platform is designed to help businesses of all sizes achieve their goals. Visit our website to learn more and sign up for a free trial!

### After

> We launched today. Here is why it almost did not happen.

> Three weeks ago, our beta testers told us the onboarding was confusing. Not "could be improved" -- confusing. 40% dropped off before completing setup.

> We had two options: launch on schedule with a known problem, or delay 3 weeks and fix it.

> We delayed. Rebuilt onboarding from scratch. Cut setup from 12 steps to 4. Drop-off went from 40% to 8%.

> The product is live now. It helps small teams automate their client reporting -- what used to take 3 hours takes 20 minutes.

> But the real lesson: your launch date is not a promise to the market. It is a guess. Ship when the product is ready, not when the calendar says so.

### What Changed

Replaced "thrilled to launch" with a story about what almost went wrong. Cut every piece of jargon ("cutting-edge," "leverages," "best-in-class," "innovative platform"). Added specific numbers that show the real development process (40% drop-off, 12 steps to 4). Named what the product actually does in plain language instead of "helps businesses achieve their goals." Ended with a transferable lesson instead of a sales pitch.

## Example 8: Vague Industry Commentary to Specific Founder Perspective

### Before

> The future of work is changing rapidly. With advancements in AI and automation, companies need to adapt or risk being left behind. It's crucial for leaders to stay ahead of the curve and embrace digital transformation. The organizations that thrive will be those that invest in their people and technology. What are your thoughts on the future of work?

### After

> I replaced a 40-hour/week role with an AI workflow last month.

> Not a person. A role. The person who was doing that work? She now runs our client strategy -- something she is actually great at and that AI cannot do.

> The role she left behind: pulling data from 4 sources, formatting weekly reports, and sending them to 23 clients. Repetitive. Error-prone. Soul-crushing.

> Here is what I learned: AI does not replace people. It replaces tasks that should not have been done by people in the first place.

> If you manage a team, audit one role this week. Not to cut headcount -- to find the 10 hours of robot work hiding inside a human job.

### What Changed

Replaced abstract industry commentary ("the future of work is changing") with a specific, first-person example with real details (40 hours, 4 data sources, 23 clients). Addressed the fear of AI replacing jobs head-on with an honest account of what actually happened. Ended with a concrete, actionable recommendation ("audit one role this week") instead of a vague question about the future. The nuance of "not a person, a role" shows genuine thinking rather than parroting a trend.
