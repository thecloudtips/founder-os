# Hook Templates: Concrete Examples by Formula and Audience

Detailed hook examples for LinkedIn post generation. Each example includes the hook text, formula type, target audience, recommended framework pairing, and a brief explanation of why it works.

---

## Stat-Led Formula

### Example 1

**Hook:**
> 87% of founders spend their first hour on email.
>
> Only 4% say it's the best use of their time.

**Formula:** Stat-Led
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** How-To, Listicle
**Why it works:** The juxtaposition between 87% doing it and 4% valuing it creates instant cognitive dissonance. The reader needs to know the alternative.

### Example 2

**Hook:**
> We reduced deployment failures by 91%.
>
> The fix wasn't better testing. It was deleting 40% of our test suite.

**Formula:** Stat-Led
**Audience:** Technical
**Framework pairing:** Contrarian Take, Personal Lesson
**Why it works:** The stat is impressive, but the counterintuitive cause (fewer tests = fewer failures) is the real scroll-stopper. Technical audiences respect specificity and will read to verify the claim.

### Example 3

**Hook:**
> Our best-performing ad last quarter had zero design assets.
>
> Plain text. White background. 3.2x ROAS.

**Formula:** Stat-Led
**Audience:** Marketer
**Framework pairing:** Contrarian Take, Personal Lesson
**Why it works:** Marketers invest heavily in creative production. The idea that plain text outperformed designed assets challenges their daily work and demands explanation.

## Question Formula

### Example 4

**Hook:**
> What would you build if you had 10 extra hours every week?
>
> Not 10 extra hours of meetings. 10 hours of deep, uninterrupted building time.

**Formula:** Question
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** How-To, Personal Lesson
**Why it works:** The clarifying second line transforms a generic aspiration into something viscerally desirable. Founders drowning in meetings feel this immediately.

### Example 5

**Hook:**
> What if 60% of your Kubernetes pods are running for no reason?
>
> We audited ours. They were.

**Formula:** Question
**Audience:** Technical
**Framework pairing:** How-To, Industry Insight
**Why it works:** Asks a question that triggers immediate anxiety, then confirms the worst case is real. Engineers will read to see the audit methodology and compare it to their own infrastructure.

### Example 6

**Hook:**
> What's the one metric your board actually cares about?
>
> I asked 23 CEOs. The answer wasn't revenue.

**Formula:** Question
**Audience:** Corporate-CXO
**Framework pairing:** Industry Insight, Listicle
**Why it works:** CXOs live in board dynamics. The specificity of "23 CEOs" signals primary research, and withholding the answer creates an irresistible gap.

## Story Formula

### Example 7

**Hook:**
> Last Thursday, I almost sent a $180,000 proposal to the wrong client.
>
> The mistake I caught taught me more about systems than any book on operations.

**Formula:** Story
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** Personal Lesson, Story
**Why it works:** The near-miss with a specific dollar amount creates stakes. "Almost" keeps it relatable rather than catastrophic, and the pivot to "systems" promises actionable takeaway, not just confession.

### Example 8

**Hook:**
> My marketing manager quit on a Tuesday.
>
> By Friday, our pipeline was up 15%. Here's what that taught me about single points of failure.

**Formula:** Story
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** Personal Lesson, Contrarian Take
**Why it works:** The expected outcome is pipeline collapse. The reversal (pipeline went up) is genuinely surprising and raises uncomfortable questions about role necessity that founders navigate constantly.

### Example 9

**Hook:**
> I spent three weeks building a custom analytics dashboard.
>
> Then an intern found the same data in a Google Sheet we already had.

**Formula:** Story
**Audience:** Technical
**Framework pairing:** Personal Lesson, Story
**Why it works:** Every engineer has over-built something. The specificity of "three weeks" and the deflation of "an intern found it" makes this painfully relatable. The humor softens the vulnerability.

## Contrarian Formula

### Example 10

**Hook:**
> Stop A/B testing your landing pages.
>
> At your traffic level, the results are statistically meaningless.

**Formula:** Contrarian
**Audience:** Marketer
**Framework pairing:** Contrarian Take, Industry Insight
**Why it works:** A/B testing is gospel in marketing. Challenging it with a statistical argument (sample size) hits marketers where they are most data-literate, making the contrarian claim hard to dismiss without reading the full argument.

### Example 11

**Hook:**
> Stop hiring senior engineers.
>
> Your codebase doesn't need seniority. It needs consistency.

**Formula:** Contrarian
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** Contrarian Take, Personal Lesson
**Why it works:** Hiring senior engineers is default founder behavior. The reframe from "seniority" to "consistency" is a genuinely useful mental model shift that founders can evaluate against their own team.

### Example 12

**Hook:**
> Forget about thought leadership.
>
> Your clients don't need your thoughts. They need your systems.

**Formula:** Contrarian
**Audience:** Corporate-CXO
**Framework pairing:** Contrarian Take, Industry Insight
**Why it works:** "Thought leadership" is deeply embedded in CXO vocabulary. Dismissing it with the direct "they need your systems" reframe is specific enough to feel actionable, not just provocative.

## Bold Claim Formula

### Example 13

**Hook:**
> The best founders I know all do the same thing before 7 AM.
>
> It's not meditation. It's not email. It's the most boring habit you'll ever adopt.

**Formula:** Bold Claim
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** Listicle, Personal Lesson, How-To
**Why it works:** "The best founders I know" establishes social proof. Eliminating the expected answers (meditation, email) narrows the gap and increases curiosity. "Most boring habit" is a pattern interrupt within the bold claim -- boringness is not usually sold as a feature.

### Example 14

**Hook:**
> The most underpriced service in B2B right now is fractional CFO work.
>
> At $2-4k/month, it's the highest-ROI hire a founder under $5M ARR can make.

**Formula:** Bold Claim
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** Industry Insight, How-To
**Why it works:** Specificity on price range and revenue threshold signals real market knowledge. Founders under $5M ARR -- a large LinkedIn audience -- immediately evaluate whether this applies to them.

### Example 15

**Hook:**
> Every company I've consulted for in the last two years has the same Slack problem.
>
> It's not notification overload. It's channel sprawl, and it's costing more than anyone measures.

**Formula:** Bold Claim
**Audience:** Corporate-CXO
**Framework pairing:** Industry Insight, Contrarian Take
**Why it works:** "Every company" is a strong universal claim that CXOs will read to either validate or challenge. Naming "channel sprawl" as the specific (and less obvious) problem differentiates this from generic Slack complaints.

## Pattern Interrupt Formula

### Example 16

**Hook:**
> Delete your to-do list.
>
> Not tomorrow. Right now. I'll explain.

**Formula:** Pattern Interrupt
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** Contrarian Take, Personal Lesson
**Why it works:** The command is short, unexpected, and actionable. "Right now" increases urgency. "I'll explain" is the minimal curiosity bridge -- just enough to justify tapping "see more."

### Example 17

**Hook:**
> Fire your best-performing sales rep.
>
> (Don't actually do it. But read this before their next 1:1.)

**Formula:** Pattern Interrupt
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** Contrarian Take, Story
**Why it works:** The retraction in the second line transforms shock into curiosity. The reader knows it is a setup but still wants to understand the insight. The parenthetical creates an intimate, conversational tone.

### Example 18

**Hook:**
> Your CI pipeline is lying to you.
>
> Green checks. Zero confidence. Here's how to tell the difference.

**Formula:** Pattern Interrupt
**Audience:** Technical
**Framework pairing:** How-To, Industry Insight
**Why it works:** "Lying to you" personifies a tool engineers trust implicitly. "Green checks. Zero confidence." is a three-word diagnosis that every engineer who has been burned by flaky tests will recognize instantly.

### Example 19

**Hook:**
> Unsubscribe from every marketing newsletter.
>
> Then rebuild your list with these 5 criteria. The signal-to-noise ratio changes everything.

**Formula:** Pattern Interrupt
**Audience:** Marketer
**Framework pairing:** Listicle, Contrarian Take
**Why it works:** Telling marketers to unsubscribe from marketing content is self-aware and unexpected. The immediate follow-up with "5 criteria" promises a rebuild framework, turning destruction into construction.

## Mixed Formula Examples (Advanced)

### Example 20 (Stat-Led + Story)

**Hook:**
> I've sent 2,400 cold emails this year.
>
> 11 turned into clients. Here's the template that landed 9 of the 11.

**Formula:** Stat-Led + Story
**Audience:** Founder-CEO
**Framework pairing:** How-To, Personal Lesson
**Why it works:** Combines the credibility of volume (2,400) with the vulnerability of a low conversion rate (11/2,400). The offer of the specific template that drove 82% of wins is an irresistible value promise.

### Example 21 (Question + Contrarian)

**Hook:**
> Why are we still building annual marketing plans?
>
> The market moves in 6-week cycles. Your plan expired before Q1 ended.

**Formula:** Question + Contrarian
**Audience:** Marketer
**Framework pairing:** Contrarian Take, Industry Insight
**Why it works:** The question challenges an entrenched practice, and the follow-up delivers a specific alternative timeframe (6-week cycles) that gives the contrarian take a constructive direction. Marketers in the middle of annual planning will feel this acutely.

### Example 22 (Bold Claim + Pattern Interrupt)

**Hook:**
> The org chart is dead.
>
> The best-run companies I advise replaced theirs with a decision rights matrix. Most teams don't need new structures. They need clarity on who decides what.

**Formula:** Bold Claim + Pattern Interrupt
**Audience:** Corporate-CXO
**Framework pairing:** Industry Insight, How-To
**Why it works:** "The org chart is dead" is a bold declaration that CXOs cannot ignore because org design is core to their role. Introducing "decision rights matrix" as the replacement gives the hook substance beyond provocation.

## Quick-Reference: Formula-Audience Matrix

| Formula | Founder-CEO | Technical | Marketer | Corporate-CXO |
|---------|-------------|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Stat-Led | Revenue, time savings, team metrics | Performance benchmarks, latency, uptime | Conversion, engagement, ROAS | Market share, growth rates, retention |
| Question | "What would you build if..." | "What if your infrastructure..." | "What's the real cost of..." | "What is your board asking about..." |
| Story | Near-miss business moments | Over-engineering confessions | Campaign wins and failures | Strategic pivot narratives |
| Contrarian | Challenge hiring/scaling norms | Challenge popular tools/practices | Challenge marketing gospel | Challenge org/strategy conventions |
| Bold Claim | "The best founders I know..." | "The best engineering teams..." | "Top-performing campaigns..." | "The companies outperforming peers..." |
| Pattern Interrupt | "Delete/Fire/Cancel your..." | "Your [tool] is lying to you" | "Unsubscribe from..." | "The [sacred concept] is dead" |
